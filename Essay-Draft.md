# Manufacturing the Quantum City: Platform-Mediated State Entrepreneurialism and Aesthetic Exclusion on Shanghai's Fuxing Island

*Draft essay — Urban Studies, Aalto University*

---

## Introduction

In December 2024, the Shanghai municipal government unveiled the "Shanghai Quantum City Space-Time Innovation Base" on Fuxing Island (复兴岛), a 1.13-square-kilometre, crescent-shaped island in the Huangpu River that had been held as an undeveloped "strategic blank" (战略留白) for roughly fifteen years (Shanghai Municipal Government, 2024). Eight months later, the island hosted RED LAND, an immersive anime-comics-games (ACGN) event organised in partnership with the social media platform Xiaohongshu, whose single-day tickets sold out in as little as three seconds and which drew nearly fifty exhibitors across an 80,000-square-metre site (Jiemian News, 2025). By May 2026, the island's newly opened waterfront hosted a music festival — attended by an estimated ten thousand visitors over two days — accompanied by thirty thousand redeemable shopping vouchers tying the event directly to over five hundred merchants in a nearby commercial district (Shanghai Municipal Culture and Tourism Bureau, 2026). In eighteen months, an idle industrial island had been converted into a branded, monetised destination.

This essay asks: how does a state-led "activation" of vacant urban land — mediated through social media platforms and staged cultural events — produce a form of exclusion that does not rely on the classic mechanism of price-driven displacement, and what does this reveal about the limits of Western gentrification theory when applied to platform-era, state-entrepreneurial urban development in China?

I argue that Fuxing Island's transformation demonstrates a **platform-mediated form of state entrepreneurialism**: a state-owned development consortium manufactures a place-brand through a coordinated sequence of symbolic language, landmark objects, and staged events, while relying on visitors' own social-media "check-in" behaviour to generate the digital visibility that is subsequently converted into trackable commercial revenue. Because the island had a negligible pre-existing residential population, this process does not produce the classic gentrification pattern of tenant eviction. It does, however, produce a narrower form of exclusion — the conversion of residual users' everyday access into a permit system, and their absence from the benefits of the new development — that is better explained by recent theorisations of digital-era "aesthetic exclusion" than by the classic displacement literature. The essay proceeds by reviewing the relevant gentrification literature, tracing the empirical case chronologically, and then situating Fuxing Island against two comparative cases — Baltimore's Harborplace and Helsinki's state-led anti-segregation redevelopment — to identify what is structurally familiar and what is genuinely new about this case.

## Literature Review

### From rent gap to state-led, new-build gentrification

Classical accounts of gentrification begin with Smith's (1979) rent-gap theory: developers profit from redeveloping land once the gap between its current capitalised rent and its potential rent becomes large enough. Hackworth and Smith (2001) periodise this into successive "waves," culminating in a third wave marked by systemic state involvement rather than sporadic public subsidy (Shaw, 2008). Davidson (2018) extends this into an account of "new-build gentrification," in which capital reinvestment, social upgrading, landscape change, and displacement — the four classic elements of gentrification (Davidson & Lees, 2005) — occur through entirely new construction on previously non-residential land, frequently under direct state sponsorship, as in China's "state-led" redevelopment model. Fuxing Island fits this pattern closely: a fifteen-year rent gap, opened by deliberate non-investment, is closed through a single, coordinated state-led capital injection rather than piecemeal private reinvestment. The mechanics of this closure are visible in the public record: in July 2025 the listed company Bailian announced an agreement with the Yangpu District Land Reserve Centre to requisition three plots totalling roughly 151,000 square metres — a textbook instance of the state "clearing" land to enable capital reinvestment (Xiao, 2025).

### Legitimising discourse

A recurring theme in the literature is that state-led gentrification requires a legitimising discourse. Lees (2008) shows how "social mix" policy is used to reframe gentrification as a corrective to segregation rather than a displacement mechanism. Yrjänä (2026), in a Helsinki case study read for this essay, extends this argument: the City of Helsinki justifies a new wave of state-facilitated, market-oriented redevelopment through an "anti-segregation" narrative, even though the practical effect is closer to conventional gentrification. She introduces two useful concepts — the "reputational gap" (the distance between a place's stigmatised present and its imagined potential, which state actors exploit to justify intervention) and the "subsidy gap" (the initial public investment required to de-risk a site for eventual private benefit). Fuxing Island's "People's City Island" (人民城市岛) branding — one of three official positionings, alongside "Digital-Intelligence Island" and "Design-and-Art Island" (Xiao, 2025) — performs a structurally similar function: it recasts a state-led, capital-oriented redevelopment as an act of returning space "to the people."

### Symbolic politics: language, objects, acts

Savini and Dembski (2016), studying the transformation of Amsterdam-Noord from a stigmatised industrial district into a "creative city," argue that political coalitions use three types of symbols — evocative *language* (metaphors such as "the next Silicon Valley"), *objects* (iconic buildings that materialise a narrative, such as an office block built on a preserved crane rail), and *acts* (staged public events that build consensus) — to link a place's industrial past to an imagined creative future. Crucially, they conclude that this symbolic construction is "selective and instrumental," and that it coexists with rising housing costs and gentrification for existing residents. This three-part framework — itself derived from Harvey's (1989) account of entrepreneurial cities competing through the "spatial division of consumption" — offers a precise analytical tool for Fuxing Island's own rebranding.

### Digital-era exclusion: prosumption and aesthetic distinction

Existing accounts of Chinese "wanghong" (internet-celebrity) urbanism note that state-led Chinese redevelopment often lacks the classic markers of Western displacement: compensation schemes for relocated residents blunt the "sense of eviction," and consumption culture is locally inflected (Cao, forthcoming). Cao's case study of Guangzhou's Dongshankou district finds that gentrification in wanghong districts occurs not primarily through price-driven eviction but through **prosumption** — visitors simultaneously consume and produce content (check-in photographs shared on Xiaohongshu) that functions as free marketing labour, generating "digital capital" for the district — and through **aesthetic distinction**: long-standing shopkeepers and residents report being crowded out not by rising rents but by visitors who "only take photographs and do not buy anything," disturbing daily routines. Cao explicitly notes that no classic, Western-style class displacement occurred in her case. This distinction — exclusion through aesthetic and behavioural incompatibility rather than price — is the analytical lens this essay applies to Fuxing Island, where the evidence likewise does not support a strong displacement claim.

## Case Study: Fuxing Island

### Building the brand: language, objects, acts

Fuxing Island's former identity was industrial: it hosted the Zhonghua Shipyard until production activities relocated off the island in the early 2000s. Rather than being redeveloped immediately, the island was held for approximately fifteen years as a "strategic reserve," a piece of official planning *language* that framed emptiness itself as a form of held value (Xiao, 2025).

This changed in December 2024, when the municipal government unveiled the "Shanghai Quantum City Space-Time Innovation Base" on the island, alongside a dedicated key laboratory and several municipal-level incubation platforms, including the "Fuxing Smart Tower" incubator (Shanghai Municipal Government, 2024). The island was designated a base to "aggregate universities, research institutes, leading enterprises, and industry bodies." This is a clear instance of Savini and Dembski's "object" and "act" categories operating together: the unveiling ceremony is a staged *act* that legitimises the new *language* ("Quantum City," 量子城市), while the roster of prestigious partner institutions functions the way MTV Europe's 2006 arrival did for Amsterdam-Noord — a flagship presence whose association retroactively certifies the district's new identity.

Eight months later, in August 2025, the island hosted RED LAND. Verified reporting confirms that tickets sold out in seconds and that the event drew nearly fifty exhibitors — including IPs such as Pokémon, Aniplex, Honor of Kings, and Genshin Impact — across an 80,000-square-metre site (Jiemian News, 2025). The event preserved the island's industrial heritage as a stage set — most notably the Shipway Park (船台公园), the island's first opened waterfront public space, which retains the physical shipways once used to launch vessels, now repurposed as a public landscape feature (Xiao, 2025). This is precisely the kind of "object" Savini and Dembski describe: a piece of industrial infrastructure re-signified, rather than removed, to lend authenticity to the new consumption-oriented use — echoing Zukin's observation that cultural, rather than economic, value increasingly drives the preservation of old structures.

In May 2026, the island's newly completed 120,000-square-metre north waterfront public space made its public debut with the "Fuxing Island Music Festival," featuring eighteen musical acts on an 80,000-square-metre lawn and drawing an estimated ten thousand visitors, with the four A-grade scenic areas of Yangpu District together receiving nearly 250,000 visitors over the holiday period (Shanghai Municipal Culture and Tourism Bureau, 2026). The event again paired industrial heritage with contemporary popular culture, this time tied to the Chinese animation IP *A Record of a Mortal's Journey to Immortality*.

### The prosumption mechanism

What distinguishes this sequence from earlier, print- and broadcast-era place-branding (such as Amsterdam-Noord's reliance on newspaper coverage and a national television documentary) is the explicit integration of visitors' own social-media activity into the value-generation process. RED LAND and the subsequent Music Festival were both designed, from the outset, around content generation: attendees' check-in photographs and short videos, shared on Xiaohongshu, function as a form of unpaid promotional labour — precisely the mechanism Cao (forthcoming) identifies as "prosumption." Notably, RED LAND was itself descended from Xiaohongshu's 2023 "Street Life Festival" in Shanghai, which mobilised 2,000 influencers and 400 merchant brands and drew over 200,000 participants (Xiao, 2025) — indicating that this is not an isolated event but the continuation of a platform-driven methodology of urban space production. The Shanghai Culture and Tourism Bureau's own description of the island's strategy, published after the May 2026 festival, frames this candidly as a deliberate "culture-commerce-tourism integration" model, stating that "the core driver of new consumption has shifted from the traditional 'goods plus venue' model to 'experience plus content'" (Shanghai Municipal Culture and Tourism Bureau, 2026) — an unusually frank admission, from the developer's own side, of the mechanism this essay is describing.

Crucially, this visibility was converted into trackable commercial revenue: attendees of the May 2026 festival received redeemable vouchers (30,000 in total, each worth 30 RMB with no minimum purchase) usable at over five hundred merchants in the nearby Wujiaochang commercial district, alongside discounted access to hotels and cultural venues (Shanghai Municipal Culture and Tourism Bureau, 2026). This closes the loop that Cao's Dongshankou case only partially completes: in Dongshankou, prosumption raises a district's platform visibility, which merely *tends to* attract further capital; on Fuxing Island, the state-linked operator has built a direct, quantifiable conversion mechanism between visitor-generated content and merchant revenue.

### Exclusion without eviction

Fuxing Island's evidentiary record does not support a classic displacement narrative. Unlike heavily populated, centrally located wanghong districts such as Shanghai's Wukang Road — where an existing residential and commercial community was displaced by an influx of visitors — Fuxing Island had, by design, a very small residential population after fifteen years of strategic non-investment. This matters for how the case should be theorised: it is not a story of residents being priced out of homes they occupied, but of a site whose population was already minimal being further reorganised around a small number of arriving user groups.

The clearest available evidence of exclusion concerns residual users rather than a displaced resident population. A primary-source government record from the Dinghai Road Sub-district office documents three "island affairs council" (岛事会) meetings held during RED LAND, convened to negotiate traffic-control arrangements with police, the event organiser, local enterprises, and residents' representatives (Dinghai Road Sub-district, 2025). Delivery workers, water-utility employees, and residents raised concerns about being unable to travel freely; the resolution converted a previously unrestricted right of movement into a permit system, ultimately issuing 1,500 vehicle permits, 900 non-motor-vehicle permits, and 52 special-vehicle permits. This is a modest but concrete instance of what Marcuse (1985) terms displacement *pressure* rather than displacement itself: daily mobility, not residence, was what had to be renegotiated.

Independent corroboration comes from den Hartog's (2021) academic field study of the Yangpu waterfront — the district within which Fuxing Island sits — conducted through interviews and site visits between 2019 and 2021. Den Hartog found that "the migrant workers who live here seldom use the waterfront spaces, because they have almost no free time," even as the same waterfront was being promoted through public branding campaigns. This finding, produced independently of the events analysed in this essay and years before RED LAND, supports the more modest claim this essay makes: that exclusion on and around Fuxing Island operates through *time poverty and eligibility gatekeeping*, not price-driven eviction — precisely the "aesthetic distinction" mechanism Cao (forthcoming) identifies in Dongshankou, where old shopkeepers were sidelined not by rising rents but by an influx of visitors whose presence and priorities simply did not include them.

## Discussion

Three comparative cases help clarify what is structurally familiar, and what is genuinely new, about Fuxing Island's transformation.

**Harvey's (1989) Baltimore** offers the clearest historical precedent for the underlying strategy: Harvey shows how Baltimore's 1978 decision to redevelop its inner harbour for private use won the city the title "Renaissance City," even as a contemporaneous congressional report still ranked it among the poorest cities in the United States — "the triumph of image over substance." Fuxing Island's "Quantum City" and "People's City Island" branding performs a structurally similar function of image-construction. The crucial difference is that Baltimore's underlying substance problem (persistent poverty despite a glossy waterfront) is well documented, whereas the equivalent question for Fuxing Island — what happens to the small number of residual users once the branding exercise proceeds — remains only partially evidenced by the traffic-permit episode and den Hartog's time-poverty finding. This is not a weakness in the case for using Harvey's framework; it is itself an honest empirical finding: image construction can be documented in detail, while its social costs, in this instance, are comparatively modest and harder to fully verify.

**Savini and Dembski's (2016) Amsterdam-Noord** shares Fuxing Island's core symbolic mechanics — language, objects, and acts working together to convert an industrial identity into a creative one — but differs sharply in political process. Amsterdam-Noord's transformation took over two decades and was repeatedly contested: a populist local party won district elections in 2002 on an explicitly anti-development platform and slowed the process for years; a citizens' association successfully lobbied to reduce the scale of luxury housing in one sub-area. Fuxing Island's transformation, by contrast, compressed an equivalent symbolic sequence into eighteen months, driven by a single administrative-corporate chain of command with no analogous electoral or grassroots veto point. The "island affairs council" is a state-convened consultation mechanism, not an independent political check comparable to Dutch district elections. This suggests that the *mechanism* of symbolic place-branding identified by Savini and Dembski is portable across very different political systems, but the *accountability* structures surrounding it are not — a finding with implications for how gentrification theory, developed largely in liberal-democratic contexts, should be adapted for single-party, state-entrepreneurial settings (Feng, Zhang & Wu, 2026).

**Yrjänä's (2026) Helsinki** offers the closest thematic parallel: a welfare-state government using a benevolent-sounding discourse ("anti-segregation," in Helsinki; "People's City," on Fuxing Island) to legitimise state-facilitated capital investment in previously undervalued land. Yrjänä's concepts of the "reputational gap" and "subsidy gap" describe Fuxing Island's fifteen years of strategic reserve equally well: the state absorbed the holding costs of vacancy so as to later close the gap between the island's stigmatised industrial past and its branded future. Where the two cases diverge is in the visibility of harm: Yrjänä's Helsinki case documents rent increases of 8.4% and 5.3% in successive years for existing social-housing tenants, alongside "managed decline" (deliberate non-maintenance pending demolition). No comparable, quantified harm to a resident population has yet been documented for Fuxing Island — again, most plausibly because the pre-existing population was already minimal, rather than because no cost exists at all.

## Conclusion

Fuxing Island illustrates a distinctive variant of state-led gentrification: a **platform-mediated state entrepreneurialism** in which a state-owned development consortium manufactures a place-brand through the coordinated use of symbolic language, landmark objects, and staged events, while depending on visitors' own social-media prosumption to generate a form of value — digital visibility — that is then converted, through a concrete voucher-redemption mechanism, into trackable commercial revenue. This case extends existing gentrification theory in two respects. First, it demonstrates that Marcuse's classic displacement framework, and its more recent Western applications (Davidson, Lees, Yrjänä), require modification when applied to sites with minimal pre-existing populations: the relevant question shifts from "who was evicted" to "whose everyday access was reorganised, and who is excluded from the benefits of reinvestment." Second, it shows that Cao's concepts of prosumption and aesthetic exclusion — developed for a densely populated historic district — travel well to an almost-empty industrial island, suggesting these are general features of platform-era Chinese urban development rather than artefacts of any single case.

The essay's claims should be read with appropriate caution. The evidence for exclusion presented here — a temporary traffic-permit system and an independent academic finding about time poverty among nearby workers — is real but modest in scale, and should not be overstated into a displacement narrative the case does not support. Two questions remain open for further research: whether the small number of relocated households near the island's edge (twenty households, from substandard pre-1960s housing, resettled in 2022) bear any direct spatial or causal relationship to the subsequent "Quantum City" development, and whether the island's newly built subsidised housing for incoming "talent" is, in practice, accessible to the small population of pre-existing workers and residents. Answering these would sharpen, rather than currently justify, a stronger claim about exclusion. What the case does firmly establish is that a rigorous, evidence-based account of *how* a symbolic and platform-mediated brand is constructed does not require — and should not be forced into — an inflated claim about *who is harmed*, when the underlying evidence for harm is genuinely more limited than in comparable European or historically populated Chinese cases.
